How do neurons talk to each other? Each connection has a "gating variable" h that jumps up when a spike arrives, then slowly fades:   h' = (−h + spike) / τ   where τ is the decay time (GABA: 4ms, AMPA: 6ms, NMDA: 160ms). The resulting synaptic current is: I = weight × h × (E_reversal − V). E_reversal is positive for excitatory receptors (pushes V up) and negative for inhibitory ones (pulls V down).

Each yellow circle = one STN neuron. Each blue circle = one GPe neuron. V = membrane voltage, U = recovery variable.

Diagram 2
Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

The DBS electrode delivers biphasic pulses (+ then −) to avoid charge buildup in tissue. The pulse spreads across the neuron grid like a bell curve — neurons near the electrode get a strong kick, those far away barely feel it.

Diagram 3
How the Simulation Works — Step by Step

The simulation advances time in tiny steps (dt ≈ 0.05 ms). At each step, every neuron's voltage is updated based on what it receives from its neighbours and any DBS current. If voltage crosses +30 mV, the neuron "fires" (spike) and resets. The LFP (local field potential) captures the collective electrical chatter of the whole region.

Diagram 4
Analysis: What the Model Tells Us — Normal vs Parkinson's (PD)

In PD, the STN–GPe loop gets "stuck" in a pathological rhythm — neurons lock into synchronised beta-band oscillations instead of firing independently. This is what DBS is designed to break by disrupting that locked rhythm and restoring healthier, more varied activity.
